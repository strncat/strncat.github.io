<h4><b>Definition</b></h4>
<p style="text-align:center;"><img src="{{ site.url }}/assets/math/trig/radians/0.png" width="40%" class="center"></p>
The radian measure of an angle is the ratio of the arc length to the radius of the circle. 
<div>
$$
\begin{align*}
\theta = \frac{\text{arc}}{r} = \frac{s}{r}.
\end{align*}
$$
</div>

<p style="text-align:center;"><img src="{{ site.url }}/assets/math/trig/radians/1.png" width="40%" class="center"></p>
For a unit circle, The radian measure of an angle would be just the corresponding arc length. This is because the unit circle's radius is defined to be 1.
<br>
<br>
<!------------------------------------------------------------------------------------>
<h4><b>One Radian</b></h4>
<p style="text-align:center;"><img src="{{ site.url }}/assets/math/trig/radians/2.png" width="40%" class="center"></p>
The ratio $\frac{arc length}{radius}$ is 1 when both the arc length and the radius of the circle are equal and this is true for any circle of any size. So one radian is typically defined as the measure of the angle subtended from the center of a circle where the arc length is equal to the radius. 
<br>
<br>
<!------------------------------------------------------------------------------------>
<h4><b>How many radians in one revolution around the circle?</b></h4>
The ratio of the circumference ($c$) to the diameter ($d$) of the circle (any circle) is the constant $\pi$. 
<div>
$$
\begin{align*}
\pi = \frac{c}{d} = \frac{c}{2r}.
\end{align*}
$$
</div>

This means that the circumference is given by, 
	\begin{align}
	c = 2\pi r.
	\end{align}


<p style="text-align:center;"><img src="{{ site.url }}/assets/math/trig/radians/3.png" width="55%" class="center"></p>
How many arc lengths of length $r$ are there in the circumference?
<div>
$$
\begin{align*}
\frac{c}{r} = \frac{2\pi r}{r} = 2\pi.
\end{align*}
$$
</div>

We have $2\pi$ arcs of length $r$. $2\pi$ is about 6.28. So approximately we have 6.28 arcs of length $r$ that cover the entire circumference of the circle. each of these arcs correspond to angle measured at 1 radian exactly (because $r/r = 1$). This means that to go a whole rotation around the entire circle we need $2\pi * 1 = 2\pi$ radians!
<p style="text-align:center;"><img src="{{ site.url }}/assets/math/trig/radians/4.png" width="50%" class="center"></p>


<!------------------------------------------------------------------------------------>
<h4><b>Radians and Degrees</b></h4>
What is the relationship between degrees and radians? We know a whole rotation around the circle is exactly 360 degrees. Therefore 360 is equivalent to $2\pi$ or in other words, 1 radian is equal to 57.2958 degrees and that's where the conversion formula between degrees and radians come from!
